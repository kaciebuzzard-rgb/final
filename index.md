Excerpt One

<h2>
The Through-Line
</h2>

<div class="memoir-copy">

<p>
There is a through-line in my life.
</p>

<p>
A small vinyl chair in a PICU room.
A woman who sits.
Who refuses to leave.
Who absorbs impact so others don’t have to.
</p>

<p>
For years, love looked like vigilance.
Advocacy. Logistics. Fighting systems.
Staying awake when everyone else slept.
</p>

<p>
Love looked like endurance.
</p>

<p>
With Jim, love looked different.
</p>

<p>
It looked like steadiness.
</p>

<p>
It looked like someone saying,
“You don’t have to carry this alone,”
and proving it in ordinary ways —
checking in, showing up, learning my children,
remembering the hard dates,
noticing when I was fading.
</p>

<p>
It looked like a man who wasn’t intimidated
by my past.
Not threatened by my competence.
Not unsettled by my complexity.
</p>

<p>
It looked like someone who didn’t need me
to shrink in order to stay.
</p>

<p>
In the space of a few short months,
we hadn’t just fallen in love.
</p>

<p>
We had built something.
</p>

<p>
A partnership.<br>
A friendship.<br>
A home I could feel myself resting inside.
</p>

</div>

</article>

Excerpt Two
</p>

<h2>
The Hospital Hallway Where Time Collapses
</h2>

<div class="memoir-copy">

<p>
There are hospital hallways that live in the body
long after you leave them.
</p>

<p>
Even now, certain light can take me back there —
that flat fluorescent glare,
the kind that erases the hour and makes midnight
feel exactly like 3 p.m.
</p>

<p>
The air always too cold.
The chairs always designed for waiting,
never for resting.
</p>

<p>
The sound of wheels.
Alarms.
Footsteps.
Voices lowered just enough
to suggest something might be wrong.
</p>

<p>
In those places, time does not move forward.
It folds in on itself.
</p>

<p>
Five minutes can hold an entire life.
A single sentence from a doctor
can split the world into before and after.
</p>

</div>

</article>

<article class="memoir-entry" id="invisible-tax">

<p class="memoir-label">
Excerpt Three
</p>

<h2>
The Invisible Tax of Invisible Labor
</h2>

<div class="memoir-copy">

<p>
People see the appointment.
They do not see the architecture behind the appointment.
</p>

<p>
They see the child arrive.
They do not see the insurance call,
the rescheduled work,
the school communication,
the medication refill,
the form that needed to be printed,
signed,
scanned,
uploaded,
and followed up on twice.
</p>

<p>
They see whether you made it.
They do not see what it cost you to get there.
</p>

<p>
That is the invisible tax of invisible labor —
not just the work itself,
but the way it disappears while still extracting
from you.
</p>

<p>
Time.
Attention.
Earnings.
Sleep.
Credibility.
Marriage.
Identity.
</p>

<p>
Caregiving is often described as emotional labor.
And it is.
</p>

<p>
But it is also systems labor.
Administrative labor.
Anticipatory labor.
</p>

<p>
It is project management under duress,
performed inside love,
with consequences for failure that are too high.
</p>

</div>
