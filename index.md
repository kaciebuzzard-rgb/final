      <p class="memoir-label">Excerpt One</p>
      <h2>The Through-Line</h2>

      <div class="memoir-copy">
        <p>There is a through-line in my life.</p>

        <p>
          A small vinyl chair in a PICU room. A woman who sits. Who refuses to leave.
          Who absorbs impact so others don’t have to.
        </p>

        <p>
          For years, love looked like vigilance. Advocacy. Logistics. Fighting systems.
          Staying awake when everyone else slept.
        </p>

        <p>Love looked like endurance.</p>

        <p>With Jim, love looked different.</p>

        <p>It looked like steadiness.</p>

        <p>
          It looked like someone saying, “You don’t have to carry this alone,” and proving it
          in ordinary ways—checking in, showing up, learning my children, remembering the hard
          dates, noticing when I was fading.
        </p>

        <p>
          It looked like a man who wasn’t intimidated by my past. Not threatened by my competence.
          Not unsettled by my complexity.
        </p>

        <p>It looked like someone who didn’t need me to shrink in order to stay.</p>

        <p>In the space of a few short months, we hadn’t just fallen in love.</p>

        <p>We had built something.</p>

        <p>A partnership.<br>
        A friendship.<br>
        A home I could feel myself resting inside.</p>
      </div>
    </article>

      <p class="memoir-label">Excerpt Two</p>
      <h2>The Hospital Hallway Where Time Collapses</h2>

      <div class="memoir-copy">
        <p>
          There are hospital hallways that live in the body long after you leave them.
        </p>

        <p>
          Even now, certain light can take me back there—that flat fluorescent glare,
          the kind that erases the hour and makes midnight feel exactly like 3 p.m.
          The air always too cold. The chairs always designed for waiting, never for resting.
          The sound of wheels, alarms, footsteps, voices lowered just enough to suggest
          something might be wrong.
        </p>

        <p>
          In those places, time does not move forward. It folds in on itself.
          Five minutes can hold an entire life. A single sentence from a doctor can split
          the world into before and after.
        </p>

        <p>
          You learn to live in fragments there. Test results. Shift changes. Intake questions.
          Medication schedules. The unbearable choreography of hope and dread.
        </p>

        <p>
          You become fluent in details nobody should have to master: oxygen numbers,
          discharge instructions, side effects, the hierarchy of specialists, who to call,
          when to push, when not to leave, when to become impossible to ignore.
        </p>

        <p>
          The hallway is where you learn that love is not always soft.
          Sometimes it is procedural. Administrative. Relentless.
          Sometimes love is keeping track. Asking again. Standing in a doorway until someone answers.
        </p>

        <p>
          Sometimes love is understanding that if you are not the one holding the thread,
          everything can come undone.
        </p>

        <p>
          And so you stand there, between machines and memory, between expertise and instinct,
          between the child you brought in and the child you pray to take home,
          while time collapses around you and still asks you to function.
        </p>
      </div>
    </article>

    <article class="memoir-entry" id="invisible-tax">
      <p class="memoir-label">Excerpt Three</p>
      <h2>The Invisible Tax of Invisible Labor</h2>

      <div class="memoir-copy">
        <p>
          People see the appointment. They do not see the architecture behind the appointment.
        </p>

        <p>
          They see the child arrive. They do not see the insurance call, the rescheduled work,
          the school communication, the medication refill, the form that needed to be printed,
          signed, scanned, uploaded, and followed up on twice.
        </p>

        <p>
          They see whether you made it. They do not see what it cost you to get there.
        </p>

        <p>
          That is the invisible tax of invisible labor: not just the work itself,
          but the way it disappears while still extracting from you.
          Time. Attention. Earnings. Sleep. Credibility. Marriage. Identity.
          The private reserves of tenderness and executive function that other people
          get to spend elsewhere.
        </p>

        <p>
          Caregiving is often described as emotional labor, and it is.
          But it is also systems labor. Administrative labor. Anticipatory labor.
          It is project management under duress, performed inside love,
          with consequences for failure that are too high.
        </p>

        <p>
          Families become case managers, schedulers, compliance officers, transportation coordinators,
          benefits specialists, record keepers, medical translators, behavior analysts,
          and crisis responders—often without training, without relief, and without being believed
          about the complexity of what they are carrying.
        </p>

        <p>
          The labor is invisible partly because it happens in fragments.
          Five minutes here. Twelve there. Forty-seven browser tabs open.
          A note in your phone. A call placed from a parking lot. A spreadsheet in your head.
          A thousand acts of maintenance so life does not collapse.
        </p>

        <p>
          And when you make it look seamless, people call you strong.
          They rarely call you overextended. They rarely call the system what it is:
          dependent on unpaid expertise it never intended to honor.
        </p>
      </div>
    </article>
